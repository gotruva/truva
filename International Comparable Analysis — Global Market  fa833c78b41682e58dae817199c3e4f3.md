## 1. MoneySavingExpert (UK) 🇬🇧

**Founded:** 2003 | **Exit:** Acquired by MoneySuperMarket for £87M | **Founder:** Martin Lewis

### What They Built

Martin Lewis started with a single manually curated page of best savings rates. No fancy tech. The edge was radical consumer advocacy — writing with conviction that banks were ripping people off. Readers trusted him because he was outraged *on their behalf*.

### What Is Directly Relevant

- Their "Best Savings" table is structurally identical to our Module 1
- The real weapon was their **weekly email newsletter** — 15M+ subscribers at peak. Every week: *"rates changed, here's what moved, here's what to do."* That email is why users came back every week instead of once
- Lewis built authority by being a consumer advocate, not a comparison engine. The product tone was: *"Banks are ripping you off. Here's the proof and here's what to do."*

### What to Emulate for [TBA]

> **Action 1:** Launch "The Truva Brief" weekly email from Day 1. Even with 100 subscribers, it trains the habit. Format: *"Maya dropped their promo rate. UNO quietly raised theirs. Here’s the updated top 3."* This is the single best retention mechanic and it costs nothing.
> 

> **Action 2:** Our positioning should carry outrage on behalf of the user: *"Most Filipinos are earning 3% when they could be earning 9%. Here's why and how to fix it."* This is a mission, not a product description.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

Lewis monetized very late and very carefully. We don't have that luxury — launch premium at Month 3 as planned.

## 2. Canstar (Australia) 🇦🇺

**Founded:** 2004 | **Status:** Profitable, privately held | **Model:** Star ratings + affiliate + B2B

### What They Built

The most architecturally similar company to [TBA]. They compare savings accounts, term deposits, credit cards, and home loans using a proprietary star-rating system. Banks pay to be "Canstar-rated." Their savings comparison table is exactly our Module 1, at scale.

### What Is Directly Relevant

Canstar cracked the **proprietary scoring system** early. Instead of just showing raw rates, they created a composite 1–5 star rating per product factoring in rate, conditions, fees, and flexibility. This does three things:

1. Makes a complex table scannable in 3 seconds
2. Positions them as the expert, not just the aggregator
3. Gives banks something to *want* — they'll pay for featured placement or to improve their score

### What to Emulate for Truva

> **Action 3: Build a** "Palago Score" (or branded equivalent) — a composite score per product. Suggested formula:
> 

> - After-tax yield: 40%
> 

> - Liquidity / withdrawal flexibility: 25%
> 

> - Conditions complexity penalty: 20%
> 

> - PDIC safety: 15%
> 

> 
> 

> This becomes our defensible IP. Lemoneyd can copy a rate table. They cannot copy a scoring methodology.
> 

> **Action 4:** The B2B pitch to Maya or UNO becomes: *"Our 45,000 users see your Palago Score. Here's how to improve it — and here's our featured placement rate."* This is the same model Canstar uses to charge banks.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

Canstar expanded too broadly too fast (insurance, home loans, electricity). Stay focused on savings and yield for 18 months minimum.

## 3. SingSaver (Singapore) 🇸🇬

**Founded:** ~2015 | **Funding:** Backed by Compare Asia Group, ~$10M raised | **Market:** Singapore + SEA

### What They Built

The cleanest UX in Southeast Asian financial comparison. Their savings page is conversion-optimized to an obsessive degree — every rate card has a single CTA button that opens the bank's signup flow directly. They tracked affiliate conversion meticulously.

### What Is Directly Relevant

SingSaver operates in a market where the regulator (MAS) requires transparent fee disclosure. They responded by *over-disclosing* affiliate relationships. Every rate card says: *"We earn a referral fee if you open this account."* Counterintuitively, this increased conversion because users trusted the recommendations more.

They also built a **"Best for you" filter** — user inputs amount and time horizon, table re-sorts. That's our Module 2 calculator logic applied directly to the comparison table.

### What to Emulate for Truva

> Action 5: Add a small tooltip on every bank card: *"We earn ₱300 if you open this account. This doesn't change the rate we show — we show all rates equally."* Honesty about how you make money is a conversion accelerator, not a deterrent.
> 

> **Action 6:** Don't make the calculator a separate page. **Embed calculator logic into the comparison table.** Amount + time horizon inputs sit above the table, and the table re-sorts to show personalized after-tax earnings per bank. The calculator IS the table.
> 

> **Action 7:** Build referral link flow properly from Day 1 — UTM parameters + pre-filled referral code passed through to the bank's page. This makes affiliate conversion tracking airtight.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

They raised VC money and expanded to 7 countries prematurely. Build dominant PH-first first.

## 4. Cermati (Indonesia) 🇮🇩

**Founded:** ~2015 | **Funding:** Series B | **Market:** Indonesia (structurally similar to PH)

### What They Built

Indonesia's financial comparison platform for a market with high mobile penetration, fragmented banking, low financial literacy — structurally very similar to Philippines. They cover savings, loans, credit cards, and insurance.

### What Is Directly Relevant

Cermati's growth story is instructive because they grew in a market with the same trust challenges as PH. Users initially assumed comparison sites were paid to recommend specific banks. Cermati broke through by investing in **financial education content that fully answered the question in the article, no gating.** This drove SEO, which drove signups.

They also built **comparison widgets** that other finance blogs could embed — distributing their rate data to other sites' audiences in exchange for backlinks.

### What to Emulate for Truva

> Action 8: Answer the question completely in every article — don't gate the answer behind a signup. *"Best digital bank rate PH March 2026 — Maya: 15% (conditions apply), UNO: 5.75%..."* Give the answer in the article. The calculator CTA comes *after* the user has already gotten value. Google rewards pages that fully answer the query. Signups follow.
> 

> **Action 9:** Before selling the API (Month 6+), offer a **free embeddable rate widget** to PH finance blogs ([Grit.ph](http://Grit.ph), JuanInvestor, FintechNews). They get fresh data, we get SEO equity and backlinks. This is pre-B2B distribution at zero cost.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

They got into loans and credit cards too early and hit OJK regulatory complexity. Stay in the information lane.

## 5. Scripbox (India) 🇮🇳

**Founded:** 2012 | **Scale:** ₹100M+ AUM managed | **Model:** Recommendation engine → licensed wealth platform

### What They Built

Started as a simple answer to *"where should I put my savings?"* — initially just recommending 3 mutual funds based on time horizon. No comparison table. Just a clear recommendation engine. Grew to become one of India's most trusted digital wealth platforms.

### What Is Directly Relevant

Scripbox's genius was **reducing choice** instead of increasing it. Most comparison sites show 20 options. Scripbox showed 3 — the best 3 for your situation. Users found this radically more useful because decision fatigue is real.

Scripbox's retention secret: **"Your money is sleeping."** They sent periodic emails telling users exactly how much they were underearning — a specific peso number, not vague advice.

### What to Emulate for [TBA]

> **Action 10:** After every calculator result, don't show all 12 banks. Show **"Your Top 3" — ranked, one sentence each, clear #1 recommendation.** Full table accessible via "See all options," but default view is the recommendation. Apply this to the PDIC Smart Split output too.
> 

> **Action 11:** Build the **"sleeping money" email trigger** into the TD Tracker. After a user enters their bank and balance: *"Your ₱200,000 in BDO earned ₱3,200 last year. In Maya, it would have earned ₱28,000. That's ₱24,800 you didn't earn."* Specific number. Not vague advice. This is the highest-converting retention email we can send.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

Scripbox eventually became a licensed investment advisor and took custody of funds. We have correctly scoped ourselves out of that lane entirely.

## 6. NerdWallet (USA) 🇺🇸

**Founded:** 2009 | **Scale:** NASDAQ-listed (NRDS), 23M+ monthly users, $379.6M revenue (2021) | **Model:** Affiliate commissions + content SEO + NerdWallet+ subscription ($49/year, added 2024)

### What They Built

Tim Chen started NerdWallet in 2009 with $800 and a single credit card comparison page. No VC. No team. Just a table and affiliate links. It grew to 30M monthly users by 2014 on SEO and content alone. Today it covers 8 verticals — credit cards, mortgages, banking, investing, insurance, personal loans, SMB, student loans — and is the definitive global blueprint for how a savings/comparison platform scales over 10+ years. Their arc is precisely the arc Truva is building: enter with one underserved vertical, earn trust through content and tools, expand into adjacent affiliate categories.

### Why Their Mobile UX Is Worth Studying

NerdWallet's mobile experience is the clearest reference point for how to handle rate comparison on small screens. The key design decisions that make it work:

1. **Cards replace tables on mobile.** Desktop shows a full comparison table with all columns. Mobile converts each product into a vertical, scrollable card: rate/offer headline at the top (large, bold), a "Best for X" label, 2–3 key specs, and a single CTA button. Users scroll through a card list rather than scrolling horizontally through a cramped table. Horizontal table scrolling on mobile is a known UX antipattern — NerdWallet avoids it entirely.
2. **Filter/sort tabs stay sticky.** Category navigation pins to the top of the screen as the user scrolls the card list. Users can re-filter without losing their place.
3. **Pre-qualification flow before results.** On high-intent pages, NerdWallet asks 2–3 questions first (credit score range, card goal, time horizon) before showing results. The card list is then personalized to the user's situation — which increases affiliate CTR significantly because users see options relevant to them.
4. **Progressive disclosure on cards.** Cards surface only what's needed to decide whether to click — headline rate, best use case, 1–2 standouts. Full details are behind an expand or modal. No wall of text at the card level.
5. **Table of contents on long pages.** Pages are content-rich (comparison table + deep editorial below). A sticky table of contents on mobile lets users jump to sections without endless scrolling.
6. **"Currency" design system.** NerdWallet's internal design system, overhauled for accessibility and consistency: clean white backgrounds, strong typography hierarchy, blue CTAs, generous white space. Feels like a trusted institution, not a startup.

### What to Emulate for Truva

> **Action 12 — Mobile: Cards, not tables.** On screens under 768px, convert the rate comparison table into a vertical card list. Each card shows: bank name + logo, after-tax rate as the headline (large, bold), conditions in one sentence, lock-in + PDIC status as icon row, Palago Score badge, and a single "Open Account" affiliate CTA. Desktop keeps the full table. This is the NerdWallet pattern — same data, different layout, optimized per context. **Important:** Lemoneyd uses horizontal table scrolling on mobile. Truva going card-first on mobile is an immediate, visible UX advantage from Day 1.
> 

> **Action 13 — 3-question pre-qual flow on mobile.** Before showing results on mobile, ask: (1) "How much are you saving?" [₱50K / ₱100K–500K / ₱500K–1M / ₱1M+], (2) "How long can you lock it in?" [Liquid / Up to 3 months / 6–12 months], (3) "Risk tolerance?" [Safe only / Open to DeFi]. Re-sort the card list based on answers. 30–60 minutes of dev work. Dramatically increases affiliate conversion — users see a list personalized to them, not a generic sorted table.
> 

> **Action 14 — Sticky category filter tabs on mobile.** When the user scrolls the card list, the category tabs (All / Banks / Govt / UITFs / DeFi) stay pinned to the top. Single CSS `position: sticky` implementation. The difference between polished mobile UX and a frustrating one.
> 

### What NOT to Copy

NerdWallet's content volume requires 200+ writers producing daily editorial. Don't try to match breadth — own 10 articles with depth and live data instead of 100 shallow articles.

### The Expansion Arc (The Truva Roadmap in Miniature)

NerdWallet started with one vertical (credit cards) → added banking → added mortgages → added investing → added SMB → reached $379.6M revenue. Every vertical followed the same formula: SEO content + comparison table + affiliate links. Truva's plan mirrors this exactly: savings first (our entry gap) → credit cards (Month 6+, 10–20x the CPA) → personal loans (Year 2). The arc is proven. The only question is execution speed.

## Key Pattern Across All 6 Companies

Every successful comparable followed the same arc:

1. **Free rate/comparison content** as the acquisition hook (SEO + trust)
2. **Calculator or personalization layer** as the upgrade trigger
3. **Affiliate commissions from institutions** as primary revenue
4. **Email alerts and rate-change notifications** as the retention loop
5. **B2B data licensing / ratings** as the revenue ceiling-raiser

Truva's current plan already follows this arc. The 11 actions above are optimizations to execute *within* the existing plan, not pivots away from it.
